1. Confidence Intervals

Definition

A confidence interval (CI) is a range of values calculated from sample data that is believed to contain an unknown population parameter with a specified level of confidence. It provides both a point estimate and a measure of uncertainty around that estimate.

Correct Interpretation

What it means: A 95% confidence interval means that if you repeated the sampling procedure 100 times and calculated a CI each time, approximately 95 of those intervals would contain the true population parameter.

What it does NOT mean: There is NOT a 95% probability that the parameter lies in this specific interval. The parameter either is or isn't in any given interval‚Äîit's fixed, not random.

Example 1: Confidence Interval for Average Height

Scenario: You survey 100 college students and find their average height is 172 cm with a standard deviation of 8 cm.

Calculation (95% CI):

This doesn't mean there's a 95% chance the true mean is in this interval‚Äîthe true mean is fixed. Rather, our method would produce intervals containing the true mean 95% of the time if repeated.

2. Steps in Hypothesis Testing

Hypothesis testing is a structured seven-step process used to make statistical decisions based on sample evidence. Here's the systematic approach:

Set the Significance Level (Œ±): Choose your alpha level, typically 0.05 (5%). This represents your acceptable probability of making a Type I error.
Choose the Appropriate Test: Select the statistical test based on data type, sample size, and assumptions (t-test, z-test, chi-square, ANOVA, etc.).
Calculate the Test Statistic: Compute the relevant statistic (t, z, F, œá¬≤) from your sample data using the appropriate formula.

Interpret Results: Explain what your decision means in the context of your research question. Avoid causal language unless it's a controlled experiment.

Consequence: Making a false claim about a finding

Type I and Type II errors have an inverse relationship. Decreasing the probability of one typically increases the probability of the other. For example, lowering Œ± from 0.05 to 0.01 reduces the chance of Type I error but increases the chance of Type II error (lower power).

Scenario: Testing if a student is guilty (H‚ÇÄ: innocent vs H‚ÇÅ: guilty)

Type I Error (False Positive):

Why the preference? Legal systems generally consider Type I error more serious, hence the high burden of proof (low Œ±). But in medical testing for serious diseases, you might prefer low Œ≤ because missing a disease (Type II) could be fatal.
